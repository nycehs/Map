DRAFT: Coronavirus in NYC

The data presented below reflect the most recent information the Health Department has collected about people who have tested positive for COVID-19 in NYC. We are discouraging people with mild to moderate symptoms from being tested at this time, so the data primarily represent people with more severe illness. All data included below are preliminary and subject to change.

This page will be updated daily.

Cases, Hospitalizations and Deaths

Daily Counts

This chart shows the number of positive cases by diagnosis date, hospitalizations by admission date and deaths by date of death from COVID-19 on a daily basis since March 3.

As of March 31, the NYC Health Department is now reporting the number of COVID-19 cases by diagnosis date, instead of report date. Diagnosis date is the date that someone went to their doctor and had a swab taken for testing. We were previously using the date the infection was reported to the Health Department. Diagnosis date is more reflective of when people are getting sick and being tested.

Due to this change, case counts per day reported previously will not match current daily counts. Information about cases over the last week will be incomplete until the laboratories and hospitals report the results for people who were tested, which can take a few days to a week.

Hover over bars to see exact values.

Inpatient Admissions Through the Emergency Department

This graph shows data on patients admitted to the hospital through the emergency room with clinical signs and symptoms consistent with COVID-19 illness, including flu-like illnesses and pneumonia. Not all of these patients will have COVID-19, but this graph can show trends of respiratory disease being reported citywide.

Cases by ZIP Code of Residence

The below map and searchable chart provide population rates of positive cases and the percent of tests with positive results. The number of reported positive cases per ZIP code may be affected by that area’s population size, access to health care and access to testing. For example, an area with a low total case number but a high percent of positive cases could reflect more people with mild symptoms not getting tested.
